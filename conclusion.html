Conclusion
Findings, LLM comparison, and directions for future work

Summary of Findings

This project demonstrated a complete knowledge engineering pipeline for Wikidata enrichment: from empirical gap discovery via SPARQL, through structured LLM prompting, to validated RDF triples. The pipeline was applied to Italian Renaissance painters with a focus on three structurally important properties: P1066 (student of), P195 (collection), and P135 (movement).

SPARQL queries against the live Wikidata endpoint confirmed that gaps are systematic rather than isolated: 25 Italian painters from 1400–1560 lack a teacher record, 26 paintings by major masters lack a museum link, and 20 painters lack an explicit art movement assignment. The gaps are concentrated among lesser-known masters — particularly painters active in Leonardo's Milanese circle, who are well-represented in art-historical scholarship but underrepresented in structured knowledge bases.

Pattern observed across prompting techniques

  • Zero-shot proved adequate for high-certainty gaps (P135 movement) but insufficient for historically ambiguous cases: both models produced confident answers for Luini's teacher without surfacing the scholarly debate around his training.
  • Few-shot most improved ChatGPT's output — format compliance increased noticeably when correct examples were shown (entity URIs, confidence labels). Gemini was already verbose and added context regardless of examples provided.
  • Chain-of-thought was the most valuable technique for P1066 and P195: by forcing explicit reasoning steps, both models acknowledged uncertainty, named competing hypotheses, and recommended verification before editing Wikidata.

The Core Problem: Plausible-Looking Errors

The most important finding is not which model performed better, but rather the category of failures that both models share: all errors were syntactically plausible but semantically wrong. A hallucinated Q-ID like wd:Q998877 looks exactly like a real identifier; a reversed triple wd:Q762 wdt:P1066 wd:Q8537 is grammatically correct Turtle but states the opposite of the intended fact. These errors cannot be caught by a Turtle parser — they require verification against the live knowledge base.

This implies that LLMs cannot yet be used as autonomous Wikidata editors. They are useful as suggestion generators, but every suggestion requires a verification step that currently demands human (or rule-based) effort.

Answer to the Research Hypothesis

The hypothesis was that chain-of-thought prompting would elicit more accurate and better-justified values than zero-shot or few-shot for historically ambiguous cases. The results confirm this: CoT was the only technique that consistently surfaced the scholarly uncertainty around Luini's teacher (no archival apprenticeship record exists), while zero-shot produced confident assertions without evidence. For P135 (art movement), where the answer is unambiguous, all three techniques performed equally well — confirming that CoT overhead is only justified for genuinely contested facts.

Future Work

  • Extend the gap analysis to all Italian Renaissance painters in Wikidata (hundreds of items), not just the 12 in this study.
  • Automated verification pipeline: after LLM suggestions are collected, automatically check each proposed Q-ID via SPARQL and flag invalid ones before human review.
  • Structured output prompting: ask models to output JSON matching the Wikidata API format, then validate the schema automatically. This would reduce the string-vs-URI error class entirely.
  • Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): supply the model with relevant Wikidata entries (retrieved by SPARQL) as context, rather than relying on parametric knowledge that may be stale or wrong.
  • Domain expert review: submit proposed triples (especially P1066 for Luini) to an art historian for validation before submitting to Wikidata.

Contribution

The project contributes: (1) a reusable SPARQL-based gap detection framework applicable to any Wikidata domain; (2) a set of 9 reusable prompt templates (zero-shot, few-shot, CoT) for Wikidata enrichment tasks; (3) an annotated before/after RDF file documenting 9 distinct error categories in LLM-generated knowledge graph data; (4) an interactive knowledge graph visualisation open-sourced via this website.
